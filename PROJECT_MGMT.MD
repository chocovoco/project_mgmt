# UNIT 1: INTRODUCTION TO PROJECT MANAGEMENT

## 1.1 Definition of Project

A **project** is defined as:

\*"A temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result"\* — \*\*PMI, PMBOK Guide\*\*



Key Characteristics of a Project:

|Characteristic|Description|
|-|-|
|**Temporary**|Definite beginning and end (not ongoing operations)|
|**Unique**|Produces something different from existing products/services|
|**Specific Objective**|Clear goal with focus on creating business value|
|**Defined Constraints**|Time, cost, scope, quality limitations|
|**Resource Consumption**|Uses human and non-human resources (money, people, equipment)|
|**Multifunctional**|Cuts across several functional lines|
|**Funding Limits**|Has budget constraints|

### Three Conditions for a Task to be a Project:

1. **Specific objective** — Must create business value within certain specifications
2. **Defined start and end dates** — Clear timeline for completion
3. **Consumes resources** — Uses money, people, equipment, materials

### Examples of Projects:

|Industry|Examples|
|-|-|
|Infrastructure|Mumbai Metro, Olympic Games, International Space Station|
|Construction|Skyscrapers, Bridges, Dams|
|Technology|Software for Banks, Website Development|
|Energy|Nuclear Plants, Solar Parks|
|Manufacturing|New Product Development|
|Publishing|Writing a Book|

## 1.7 Project Management Approaches

### 1\. Traditional Approach

* Planning, executing, controlling tasks to reach goal within budget/time
* Best when: Requirements are clear, changes are minimal

### 2\. Agile Approach

* Highly collaborative methodology with continuous feedback
* Emphasizes: Small batches, customer collaboration, quick feedback, transparency
* Best for: Software projects, evolving requirements

### 3\. Waterfall Approach

* Sequential linear steps; one phase completes before next starts
* Introduced by Winston W. Royce (1970)
* Best for: Construction, manufacturing, well-defined projects

### 4\. Systems Approach

* Visualizes and analyzes physical/conceptual systems
* Framework for: Conceptualizing problems, solving problems, making decisions

### 5\. Critical Chain \& Critical Path Approach

* **Critical Path:** Longest sequence of tasks determining project duration
* **Critical Chain:** Sequence of precedence and resource-dependent tasks

### 6\. Program Management Approach

* For handling multiple projects simultaneously
* Used for change management, industrial engineering

## 1.8 Systems Approach to Project Management

### Definition of System:

> \\\\\\\*"An organized or complex whole; an assemblage of parts interacting in a coordinated way towards a common purpose."\\\\\\\*

### Components of a System:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                         SYSTEM                                   │
│  ┌─────────┐    ┌──────────────┐    ┌──────────┐                │
│  │ INPUTS  │───▶│   PROCESS    │───▶│ OUTPUTS  │                │
│  │People   │    │Conversion    │    │Goods     │                │
│  │Materials│    │Activities    │    │Services  │                │
│  │Info     │    │              │    │Info      │                │
│  │Capital  │    │              │    │Waste     │                │
│  └─────────┘    └──────────────┘    └──────────┘                │
│       ▲                                      │                    │
│       │         ┌──────────────┐            │                    │
│       └─────────┤   FEEDBACK   │◄───────────┘                    │
│                 └──────────────┘                                 │
│                                                                   │
│  ENVIRONMENT (Economic, Legal, Political, Social)                 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Key System Concepts:

|Concept|Explanation|
|-|-|
|**Sub-System**|Operating level; departments are subsystems of company|
|**Environment (Supra System)**|External factors affecting the system|
|**System Boundary**|Outer limit of system's operations|
|**Open System**|Interacts with environment (constant input/output)|
|**Closed System**|Does not interact with environment|
|**Input-Output Conversion**|Transforming inputs to outputs|
|**Feedback**|Information about system's status and performance|
|**Synergy**|Output > sum of individual outputs (2+2=5)|

### Benefits of Systems Approach:

* Integrates classical, neoclassical, and management science elements
* Provides orderly plan of action

### Weaknesses:

* Less pragmatic and action-focused
* Doesn't explain precise direction/magnitude of change
* No unified theory for all organizations
* Lacks specific tools for executives

## 1.9 Project Feasibility

### Definition:

> \\\\\\\*"Feasibility is the process of investigating a need, problem, and solutions to determine if a solution is economically viable and worth developing."\\\\\\\*

### Types of Feasibility Studies:

|Type|Focus|
|-|-|
|**Technical Feasibility**|Manpower and technological requirements|
|**Operational Feasibility**|Will the project work in practice?|
|**Economic Feasibility**|Costs vs. benefits analysis|
|**Legal Feasibility**|Compliance with laws/regulations|
|**Schedule Feasibility**|Can project be completed on time?|

### Complete Feasibility Analysis Must Cover:

1. Technical (manpower, technology)
2. Economic justification (costs and benefits)
3. Administrative/managerial (external linkages, internal organization)
4. Environmental (baseline data, impact)
5. Social/political (demographic data, social needs)
6. Financial (funding needs and sources)

### Detailed Phases:

|Phase|Activities|Key Deliverables|
|-|-|-|
|**1. Initiation/Conception**|Explore concept, define scope, feasibility studies, assemble team, develop project charter|Project charter, PM identified|
|**2. Planning/Definition**|Detail scope, identify tasks/resources, prepare schedule/budget, identify risks, create quality plan, communication plan|Project management plan, WBS, schedule, budget|
|**3. Execution/Implementation**|Perform work outlined, consume resources/energy, monitor work efficiency, status reports, measure deliverables against metrics|Project deliverables|
|**4. Closure/Transfer**|Hand over final deliverable, terminate contracts, inform stakeholders, review project, document lessons learned|Final product, lessons learned|

### Risk vs. Value Across Life Cycle:

* **Risk** decreases as project progresses
* **Value** increases as project progresses

## 1.11 Systems Development Cycle (SDLC)

|Phase|Description|

## 1.12 Triple Constraints of Project Management

### The Three Major Constraints:

```
                    ┌─────────────┐
                    │             │
                    │    SCOPE    │
                    │  (What)     │
                    │             │
          ┌─────────┼─────────────┼─────────┐
          │         │             │         │
          │    TIME │   PROJECT   │   COST  │
          │   (When)│   GOAL      │  (How   │
          │         │             │  much)  │
          │         │             │         │
          └─────────┼─────────────┼─────────┘
                    │             │
                    │   QUALITY   │
                    │   (4th      │
                    │  constraint)│
                    └─────────────┘
```

### Relationship: Cost = f (Performance, Time, Scope)

|Relationship|Effect|
|-|-|
|**Cost vs. Scope**|Increasing scope → increases cost|
|**Cost vs. Time**|Shortening time → increases cost (overtime, extra resources)|
|**Cost vs. Performance**|Higher quality → increases cost (skilled labor, better materials)|

### Example (Software Development):

> Client requests additional features (scope increase) + faster delivery (time reduction) + high quality → Project cost increases due to additional developers, overtime, testing.

### Need to Go Beyond Triple Constraints:

|Modern Consideration|Why Important|
|-|-|
|Quality|Fourth constraint; ensures standards met|
|Stakeholder satisfaction|Key to project success|
|Risk \& uncertainty|Increasing in modern projects|
|Benefits \& value delivery|Now central to project evaluation|
|Sustainability \& compliance|Legal and environmental requirements|
|Team dynamics \& leadership|Impact organizational culture|

## 2.2 Types of Organizational Structures

### 1\. Traditional/Classical/Functional Organization

**Structure:**

```
                    ┌─────────────┐
                    │    CEO      │
                    └──────┬──────┘
                           │
           ┌───────────────┼───────────────┐

      │ Staff   │     │ Staff   │     │ Staff   │
      └─────────┘     └─────────┘     └─────────┘
```

**Characteristics:**

* Developed around functional aspects (engineering, manufacturing, marketing, HR, IS)
* Best for small organizations with few products/services

**Advantages:**

|Advantage|Description|
|-|-|
|Easier budgeting and cost control|Clear financial tracking|
|Better technical control|Specialized supervision|
|Specialists can share knowledge|Collaboration within functions|
|Personnel can be used on multiple projects|Resource flexibility|
|Continuity in functional disciplines|Stable policies/procedures|
|One reporting person per employee|Clear chain of command|
|Vertical, well-established communication|Clear channels|

**Disadvantages:**

|Disadvantage|Description|
|-|-|
|No single person responsible for total project|No formal authority|
|No project-oriented emphasis|Weak project focus|
|Complex coordination|Additional lead time for approvals|
|Decisions favor strongest functional groups|Political bias|
|No customer focal point|Poor customer response|
|Difficulty pinpointing responsibility|Unclear accountability|
|Decreased motivation and innovation|Functional orientation|

### 3\. Matrix Organization

**Structure:**

```
                    ┌─────────────┐
                    │    CEO      │
                    └──────┬──────┘
                           │
         ┌─────────────────┼─────────────────┐
         │                 │                 │
    ┌────┴────┐       ┌────┴────┐       ┌────┴────┐
    │Functional│       │Functional│       │Functional│
    │ Manager │       │ Manager │       │ Manager │
    │  (Engg) │       │ (Mktg)  │       │ (Fin)   │
    └────┬────┘       └────┬────┘       └────┬────┘
         │                 │                 │
         └─────────────────┼─────────────────┘
                           │
                    ┌──────┴──────┐
                    │ Project     │
                    │ Manager     │
                    └─────────────┘
```

**Characteristics:**

* Combines functional and projectized structures
* Each team member has two bosses (functional manager AND project manager)
* Used for large projects/product development

**Types of Matrix:**

|Type|Power Distribution|
|-|-|
|**Strong Matrix**|Power resides more with project manager|
|**Weak Matrix**|Power resides more with functional manager|
|**Balanced Matrix**|Power shared equally|

**Advantages:**

|Advantage|Description|
|-|-|
|Maximum project control through line managers|Strong oversight|
|Independent policies/procedures per project|Flexibility|
|PM can commit resources (subject to scheduling)|Authority|
|Rapid response to changes|Agility|
|Each person has "home" after project completion|Job security|
|Key people can be shared|Cost minimization|
|Strong technical base|Better problem solving|
|Minimal conflicts|Easier resolution|
|Better balance among time, cost, performance|Trade-off management|

**Disadvantages:**

|Disadvantage|Description|
|-|-|
|Multidimensional information flow|Complexity|
|Multidimensional work flow|Complexity|
|Dual reporting|Confusion|
|Continuously changing priorities|Instability|
|Potential for continuous conflict|Stress|
|Difficulty in monitoring and control|Oversight challenges|
|Not cost-effective (more people needed)|Higher costs|
|Functional managers may be biased|Priority conflicts|
|Role ambiguity|Unclear responsibilities|

**Ground Rules for Matrix Development:**

1. Participants are full-time on project (ensures loyalty)
2. Horizontal and vertical channels for commitments
3. Quick and effective conflict resolution methods
4. Good communication channels and free access
5. All managers have input into planning process
6. Managers willing to negotiate for resources
7. Horizontal line operates as separate entity (except for admin)

## 3.3 Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

## 3.6 Network Diagrams

## 3.7 Critical Path Method (CPM)

### Definition:

> \\\\\\\*"A scheduling technique whose order and duration of a sequence of task activities directly affect the completion date of a project."\\\\\\\*

### Key Concepts:

|Term|Definition|
|-|-|
|**Critical Path**|Longest sequence of tasks that determines shortest possible project duration|
|**Critical Activity**|Activity with zero total float|
|**Critical Event**|Event with zero slack|

### Forward Pass (Earliest Times):

|Formula|Description|
|-|-|
|(Es)ᵢⱼ = Eᵢ|Earliest start = Earliest time of tail event|
|(Ef)ᵢⱼ = (Es)ᵢⱼ + Dᵢⱼ|Earliest finish = Earliest start + Duration|

### Backward Pass (Latest Times):

|Formula|Description|
|-|-|
|For ending event: E = L|Earliest = Latest for end|
|(Lf)ᵢⱼ = Lⱼ|Latest finish = Latest time of head event|
|(Ls)ᵢⱼ = (Lf)ᵢⱼ - Dᵢⱼ|Latest start = Latest finish - Duration|

### Fulkerson's Rule for Numbering Events (AOA):

|Step|Action|
|-|-|
|1|Starting event numbered '1'|
|2|Move left to right|
|3|If multiple start events, number top to bottom|
|4|Last event gets highest number|
|5|No two events have same number|

## 3.8 Float Calculations

### Types of Float:

|Float Type|Formula|Description|
|-|-|-|
|**Total Float (TF)**|(Ls)ᵢⱼ - (Es)ᵢⱼ or (Lj - Ei) - Dij|Max time activity can be delayed without affecting project completion|
|**Free Float (FF)**|(Ej - Ei) - Dij|Time activity can be delayed without delaying immediate successor|
|**Independent Float (IF)**|(Ej - Li) - Dij|Time activity can be delayed without affecting preceding/succeeding activities|
|**Interfering Float**|TF - FF|Part of total float that reduces float of succeeding activities|
|**Slack**|Latest occurrence time - Earliest occurrence time|Time event can be delayed|

### Event Slack:

* Slack at node = minimum of float of all activities starting from that node

## 3.9 PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique)

### Definition:

> \\\\\\\*"A probabilistic scheduling technique used when activity durations are uncertain, typically for R\\\\\\\&D projects."\\\\\\\*

### Three Time Estimates:

|Estimate|Symbol|Description|
|-|-|-|
|Optimistic time|t₀ (or a)|Best-case scenario|
|Most likely time|tₘ (or m)|Normal scenario|
|Pessimistic time|tₚ (or b)|Worst-case scenario|

### Expected Time (Mean):

```
                    t₀ + 4tₘ + tₚ
              tₑ = ───────────────
                          6
```

### Variance:

```
                    (tₚ - t₀)²
              σ² = ───────────
                        36
```

### Standard Deviation:

```
                    (tₚ - t₀)
              σ = ───────────
                        6
```

### Project Standard Deviation:

```
        σ\\\\\\\_project = √(σ₁² + σ₂² + σ₃² + ... + σₙ²)
        
        where activities are on critical path
```

### Z-Score (Probability Calculation):

```
                    t - Tₑ
              z = ────────
                      σ
```

Where:

* t = target completion time
* Tₑ = expected project duration (sum of tₑ on critical path)

### Probability Interpretation:

|z value|Approximate Probability|
|-|-|
|z = 0|50%|
|z = 1|84.1%|
|z = 1.5|93.3%|
|z = 2|97.7%|
|z = -1|15.9%|
|z = -2|2.3%|

### Important Notes for PERT:

1. PERT uses AOA convention (event-based)
2. AON cannot be used for PERT
3. Crashing is not typically done on PERT networks
4. Normal distribution tables are used for probability

## 3.10 Crashing Analysis

### Definition:

> \\\\\\\*"The process of reducing project duration by adopting more efficient processes, equipment, or personnel at additional cost."\\\\\\\*

### Cost Components:

|Cost Type|Description|
|-|-|
|**Direct Cost**|Remains same regardless of duration (materials, labor hours)|
|**Indirect Cost**|Depends on project duration (overhead, supervision, interest)|
|**Crashing Cost**|Additional cost to reduce duration|

### Cost Slope Formula:

```
                    Cc - Cn
           Slope = ─────────
                    Tn - Tc

Where:
  Cc = Crash cost
  Cn = Normal cost
  Tn = Normal time
  Tc = Crash time
```

### Crashing Procedure:

|Step|Action|
|-|-|
|1|Calculate path duration times for all paths|
|2|Identify activities on critical path with least cost slope|
|3|Crash by one time unit|
|4|Update network and check for new critical paths|
|5|Calculate new total cost|
|6|Repeat until no further crashing possible or cost exceeds benefit|

### When Crashing is Beneficial:

* When indirect cost savings > crashing cost
* When early completion bonuses are available
* When avoiding penalties for delays

## 4.3 Cost Estimating Process

## 4.4 Cost Estimation Methods

### 1\. Analogous Estimating (Top-Down)

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Uses historical data from similar past projects|
|**Advantages**|Quick, inexpensive|
|**Limitations**|Less accurate, depends on expert judgment|

### 2\. Parametric Estimating

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Uses statistical relationships between historical data and variables|
|**Advantages**|More accurate if reliable data available|
|**Limitations**|Requires strong database and mathematical models|

### 3\. Bottom-Up Estimating

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Breaks project into components, estimates each, aggregates|
|**Advantages**|Highly accurate and detailed|
|**Limitations**|Time-consuming, resource-intensive|

### 4\. Three-Point Estimating

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Uses Optimistic (O), Most Likely (M), Pessimistic (P)|
|**Formula**|Expected = (O + 4M + P) / 6|
|**Advantages**|Accounts for uncertainty and risk|

### 5\. Expert Judgment

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Relies on insights from experienced professionals|
|**Advantages**|Useful when data limited|
|**Limitations**|Subjective|

### 6\. Reserve Analysis

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Adds contingency reserves to cover identified risks|
|**Advantages**|Helps manage uncertainty|
|**Limitations**|Can inflate budget|

### 7\. Vendor Bid Analysis

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Uses bids from suppliers/contractors|
|**Advantages**|Market-driven, realistic|
|**Limitations**|Depends on vendor reliability|

### 8\. Cost of Quality (CoQ)

|Aspect|Description|
|-|-|
|**Method**|Estimates costs related to quality (prevention, appraisal, failure)|
|**Advantages**|Helps balance quality and cost|

# UNIT 5: PROJECT RISK MANAGEMENT

## 5.1 Basic Concepts of Risk

### Definitions:

|Source|Definition|
|-|-|
|**ISO 31000**|"Risk is an effect of uncertainty on objectives"|
|**PMBOK**|"Project Risk is an uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has negative or positive effect on project objectives"|
|**Merriam-Webster**|"The possibility of loss or injury"|

### Two Key Concepts of Risk:

```
                    Risk = f (Likelihood, Impact)

        Where:
        - Likelihood = Probability the event will occur
        - Impact = Consequence if the event occurs
```

### Risk Characteristics:

* Has element of uncertainty and likelihood
* Once it happens, becomes an **issue** with consequences
* Associated with future event (hasn't happened yet)

## 5.9 Qualitative Risk Analysis

### Definition:

> \\\\\\\*"Assessment of potential risk based on subjective judgment and descriptive terms like 'high,' 'medium,' or 'low' to evaluate likelihood and impact."\\\\\\\*

## 5.10 Quantitative Risk Analysis

### Definition:

> \\\\\\\*"The process of assigning numerical values to potential risks, using data and statistical methods to assess probability and impact."\\\\\\\*

### Purpose:

* Better overall project risk analysis
* Better business decisions
* Better estimates

### When Used:

* Projects requiring contingency reserve
* Large, complex projects requiring GO/NO-GO decisions
* Projects where management wants detail about completion probability

### Tools and Techniques:

|Tool|Description|
|-|-|
|**Decision Tree Analysis**|Graphical representation of decisions and consequences|
|**Simulation (Monte Carlo)**|Generates multiple scenarios|
|**Sensitivity Analysis**|Analyzes effect of variable changes|
|**FMEA**|Failure Mode and Effect Analysis|

### FMEA Process (6 Steps):

|Step|Action|
|-|-|
|1|List possible ways project might fail|
|2|Evaluate Severity (S) on 1-10 scale|
|3|Estimate Likelihood (L) on 1-10 scale|
|4|Estimate Inability to Detect (D) on 1-10 scale|
|5|Calculate Risk Priority Number: RPN = S × L × D|
|6|Consider ways to reduce S, L, D for high RPN|

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Risk Analysis:

|Aspect|Qualitative|Quantitative|
|-|-|-|
|**Purpose**|Prioritize risks|Measure risk impact numerically|
|**Approach**|Descriptive scales (High/Medium/Low)|Numerical techniques|
|**Data Requirement**|Limited, expert opinion|Detailed, accurate data|
|**Output**|Risk ranking|Quantified risk values|
|**Tools**|Probability-Impact Matrix|Simulation, sensitivity analysis|
|**Time \& Effort**|Faster, less resource-intensive|Time-consuming|
|**When Used**|Early stages|Later stages, complex projects|

## 6.1 Project Closure/Termination

### Definition:

## 6.2 Types of Project Termination

|Type|Description|Example|
|-|-|-|
|**Termination by Extinction**|Project ends because objectives achieved or no longer needed|Successful delivery, project cancelled|
|**Termination by Addition**|Deliverables/team absorbed into parent organization permanently|IT system becomes permanent department|
|**Termination by Integration**|Resources integrated back into organization|Team members return to functional departments|
|**Termination by Starvation**|Slowly deprived of resources until can't continue|Low-priority project loses funding|
|**Termination by Suspension (Hold)**|Temporarily stopped with possibility of resuming|Construction paused for regulatory issues|

## 6.3 Steps in Project Termination

### Key Activities:

|Activity|Description|
|-|-|
|Release resources and staff|Complete staff associated with project|
|Close financial issues|Contracts, loans, procurement, clear dues|
|Collect project records from client|Complete documentation|
|Document project records for future references|Archive|
|Document issues faced and solutions|Lessons learned|
|Record lessons learned for guidelines|Future improvement|

### Three Major Categories of Closeout Activities:

#### 1\. Planning, Scheduling, and Monitoring Closeout Activities

* Obtain and approve termination plans from functional managers
* Prepare and coordinate termination plans and schedules
* Plan for transfer of team members and resources
* Monitor completion of contractual agreements
* Monitor disposition of surplus materials/equipment

#### 2\. Final Closeout Activities

* Close out all work orders and contracts
* Notify all departments of project completion
* Close project office and facilities
* Close project books
* Ensure delivery of project files/records to responsible managers

#### 3\. Customer Acceptance, Obligation, and Payment Activities

* Ensure delivery of end-items, side-items, and customer acceptance
* Notify customer when all contractual obligations fulfilled
* Ensure all documentation for customer acceptance completed
* Expedite customer activities needed to complete project
* Transmit formal payment and collect payments
* Obtain formal acknowledgment of completion from customer
